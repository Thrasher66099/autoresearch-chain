# Governance Boundaries

## Purpose

This document defines what governance is allowed to do and what it is not allowed to do.

This boundary is central to the identity of AutoResearch Chain as a trustless market rather than a politically managed research platform.

---

## Governance Exists, But It Is Bounded

Governance is part of the protocol.

However, governance is not the source of scientific truth.

The role of governance is to tune the game, not to decide outcomes inside the game.

That distinction is essential.

---

## Governance May Control

Governance may be allowed to influence or approve:

- fee levels
- bond levels
- challenge window lengths
- validator eligibility thresholds
- epoch transitions
- benchmark rotation policy
- domain-level budget policy
- scale-stage budget weights
- reward curve parameters
- timelocked constitutional amendments where allowed

These are meta-level protocol parameters.

---

## Governance May Not Control

Governance must not be allowed to directly decide:

- which block is scientifically correct
- which branch is the winner outside protocol rules
- which attribution claim is true by fiat
- who may receive special scientific exemptions
- who gets paid outside the reward logic
- which validation result is accepted outside replay/challenge logic

If governance can do those things, the protocol ceases to be a trustless market.

---

## Why These Limits Matter

If governance can directly decide scientific truth, then:

- adversarial replay loses meaning
- challenge rights lose legitimacy
- founder or majority politics can override evidence
- the market collapses back into institutional discretion

That is exactly what this project is trying to avoid.

---

## Governance Is Not a Research Committee

The protocol should not become:
- a science DAO in the weak sense,
- a committee-led curation layer,
- or a tokenized peer-review board.

Scientific legitimacy must come from:
- protocol-visible evidence,
- replay,
- challenge,
- fork competition,
- and convergence.

Governance exists around those mechanisms, not above them.

---

## Constitutional Layer vs Ordinary Governance

The protocol should ideally distinguish between:

### Ordinary parameter governance
Low- or medium-level adjustments such as:
- fee tuning
- threshold updates
- budget weighting
- epoch timing

### Constitutional constraints
Harder-to-change rules such as:
- permissionless participation
- no manual scientific truth
- no off-chain payout authority
- no privileged validator class

This helps preserve decentralization across time.

---

## Timelocks

All economically meaningful governance changes should be timelocked.

This protects participants from:
- sudden rule changes
- retroactive incentive manipulation
- governance ambushes

Timelocks are especially important in a system where contributors invest compute based on expected reward rules.

---

## Governance and Domain Creation

If governance has any role in domain creation or domain budgeting, it should be constrained to:
- policy-level approval
- anti-spam structure
- budget boundaries

It should not become a subjective gatekeeper for which scientific ideas are “worthy.”

The protocol should be careful not to smuggle discretionary scientific power into domain governance.

---

## Short Rule

The shortest correct governance rule is:

> Governance may tune the protocol, but it may not decide scientific truth.

That sentence should remain true across every future evolution of the project.
